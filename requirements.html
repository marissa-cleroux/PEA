Requirements { Objective 5 }

Business Problem

The Nursing Program has a series of skills that students are required to practice in the lab setting and then in the clinical setting after successfully practicing it in the lab setting. Before Nursing Skills Tracker (NST), students had to track their skills via paper documents throughout the course of the program. It was very difficult for teachers to determine when students could practice a skill in a clinical setting. Students also desired a way to generate a portfolio of the skills they had acquired. Teachers also wanted a way for students to keep track of when it’s time to practice a skill again so that student could be autonomous. NST solved all this by allowing students and teachers to input evaluations for skills and easily show which skills students have practiced. The original elevator pitch for NST can be found here.

Scope

Users & Other Systems

NST has several categories of users:

  • Nursing Department Coordinator;
  • Nursing Teachers;
  • Nursing Clinical Teachers (teachers who supervise students in the clinical setting);
  • Nursing Liaison Teachers (teachers who act as liaisons for students in their preceptorship); and
  • Nursing Students.

NST depends on two other systems

  • Clara (an interface which stores all student, teacher, and program data); and
  • The Heritage College Application Management System (AMS) (manages applications and authorization for Heritage College applications deployed by the Computer Science program).

Functionality

The main functionality of NST is tracking the skills gained by students in the Nursing program. Students can also generate portfolios of their acquired competencies. Teachers will use NST to leave evaluations for their students’ skills and give feedback. Teachers can also use it to track their cohorts progress and ensure individual students are on track. Coordinators will manage the skills and units available for students to evaluate as well as have the abilities of a teacher. Coordinators can also generate portfolios for any current or past students who have successfully completed skills in the clinical setting.

Business Requirements

Business requirements are requirements for a system from the perspective of the business. A functional requirement describes something that the system must do. A non-functional requirement describes how the system must be. All functional requirements are captured by user stories. Acceptance criteria conveys the test scenarios and context for the functional requirements. An example of a non-functional requirement in NST is to optimize the GetAllSkills() method so that it is faster than 18 seconds.

Based on the initial presentation by the client the team summarized the perceived requirements. We then discussed with the client if these requirements covered what she wanted and what her priorities were. We also took the opportunity to clarify any confusing specifications and refine the requirements. That meeting is summarized in these meeting minutes. After gathering the initial requirements, we implemented some functionality based on the client’s priorities and the time allocated to complete the sprint. During the sprint review meeting, the client provided feedback and had the final say on whether the requirements were met or if more work was needed; the notes are found here . The same process was then carried out for the second sprint, as summarized here.

Example Requirement

An example of, one user story that I was the main contributor for is: “As a student, I want to be able to update when I have practiced a skill.” Before this functionality was complete, a student was not able to add a new evaluation to a skill once they had been evaluated. The client wanted the student to be able to use NST to track when they should practice a skill again, and thus be able to update their evaluation every time they practice it.
